Project Sprout

Making sustainable information more accessible to architects in the design process

Some information has been omitted from this case study due to confidentiality. The views and information shown are based of my own opinions and findings, which are not necessarily representative of Foster+Partners

The Problem

The sustainability of design is becoming an increasing concern in architecture. Whilst there are many methodologies to address it, and standards to aim for, quantitative methods for analysing a buildings impact are still relatively new and are yet to penetrate the design process. Typically this would be outsourced to an external consultant, or if it wasn’t a priority, would be avoided.

My Role: Researcher, Designer and Developer

The Starting Point

EPDs (or Environmental Product Declarations) are independently verified reports conducted to ISO standards in order to evaluate a supply chains sustainability. They look at a range of environmental impacts across the lifecycle of a product in order to measure the sustainability of it.

However EPDs are technical documents, they aren't friendly to non-technically experienced users and can be difficult to understand

100 EPD's were examined, and the following issues were found:

  • The level of detail and length varied from 6 to 22 pages
  • There were inconsistencies in the functional unit applied for the calculations e.g. some would have a panel as a unit, others would have 1m3 of material
  • There was no visual consistency between EPD’s
  • There are a number of technical terms and acronyms that are rarely explained

Ultimately this meant that the important information wasn't accessible to the designers. From speaking with architects and the managers on projects, the tasks related to sifting through the information would be delegated to a handful of "experts" or not considered.


So how to improve it?


The Existing Tools

There are solutions that attempt to address these issues, but the uptake is low. From some user trials and direct feedback, the competition could be categorised into 3 types, with the following pros and cons:

Entry Level

ICE Database

+ Information and products are listed simply and clearly
- Most of the data is research based, resulting in extremely dated information
- Not all sources are listed, with limited transparency

Mid

Athena, One Click LCA, eTool LCD

+ Builds up calculations from the architectural stand point
+ Speeds up the process and reduces the technical skills needed
- Shortcuts taken can result in wildly inaccurate results
- Locality of data not necessarily relevant

In-Depth

GaBi, SimaPro

+ Has great transparency to see the origins of the data and supply chains
+ Good levels of details and accuracy, with a lot of freedom to create custom profiles
- Requires a training course or experience to use properly
- Targeted at products, not buildings

From these options, the Mid-End options were the most common in industry. They had been tailored towards architects, with the input process relating more to a building than a product. They also limited their materials data to include only typical architectural materials, reducing the stress when selecting materials.

There was a clear lack of knowledge on the topic as a whole. It would be important to walk them through the process of inputting the information

There are other metrics beyond just carbon footprint included in EPD’s, and raising awareness of these to inform decision making would be helpful

The differences in transport can have more of an impact than the material choice, which would be important to illustrate
